<h2>Changing the Direction of Global Trade: Toward a Need-Based and Mutually Beneficial System</h2><h3>1. Introduction: Problems with the Current Trade Structure</h3><p>The current international trade system is largely export-driven and supplier-centric. Countries aim to maximize exports in order to boost domestic industries and earn foreign currency. This method of trade, however, causes a range of issues:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Overproduction and resource waste</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Environmental destruction and increased carbon emissions</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Coercive export structures that pressure importers</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Trade imbalances and international tensions</strong></p>
</li>
</ul><p>This model not only prioritizes economic gains but also contributes to global environmental crises and political instability.</p><hr><h3>2. A New Direction for Trade: Transitioning to Need-Based Trade</h3><p>It is time for the international community to move from a supply-driven trade model to a <strong>demand-centered, need-based system</strong>. In simple terms, each country should only import goods that are truly needed by its people, and export goods only when another country has a clear demand for them.</p><p>The core principles of this model are as follows:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Only export based on explicit demand from the other country</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Importing countries must notify exporters in advance of quantity and type of goods</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Producers only grow or manufacture based on pre-agreed export volumes</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Eliminate forced or unilateral export pressure</strong></p>
</li>
</ul><hr><h3>3. Concrete Example: Agricultural Trade Between the U.S. and Korea</h3><p>For instance, the United States should grow agricultural products only for domestic consumption, and produce additional quantities only when there is a confirmed demand from a foreign country.</p><ul>
<li>
<p>If Korea wants to import U.S. cherries or avocados,<br>
→ The Korean government or importers submit a request to U.S. producers in advance<br>
→ Only the requested volume is produced additionally and exported<br>
→ This prevents unnecessary overproduction</p>
</li>
</ul><p>Similarly, the U.S. would only import goods from Korea that it actually needs, based on prior demand.</p><p>This forms a virtuous cycle of <strong>pre-order → production → necessary import</strong>, reducing inefficiencies.</p><hr><h3>4. Advantages of the Need-Based Trade System</h3><p>This system of need-based trade brings multiple benefits:</p><h4>1) <strong>Prevents Overproduction and Protects the Environment</strong></h4><ul>
<li>
<p>Reduces land misuse, water waste, and carbon emissions by minimizing unnecessary output</p>
</li>
</ul><h4>2) <strong>Reduces International Trade Conflicts</strong></h4><ul>
<li>
<p>Eliminates aggressive export strategies and competition for trade surpluses</p>
</li>
</ul><h4>3) <strong>Cuts Logistics and Transport Costs</strong></h4><ul>
<li>
<p>Minimizes fuel consumption and shipping costs by reducing unneeded shipments</p>
</li>
</ul><h4>4) <strong>Restores Resource Sovereignty</strong></h4><ul>
<li>
<p>Nations import only what they need, respecting domestic supply chains</p>
</li>
</ul><h4>5) <strong>Prevents Wars and Enhances Peace</strong></h4><ul>
<li>
<p>Reduces resource-based conflicts and builds trust through cooperative trade</p>
</li>
</ul><hr><h3>5. Policy Recommendations for Implementation</h3><p>To make this ideal trade system a reality, the following policies are necessary:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Reform International Trade Agreements</strong><br>
→ Amend existing systems like WTO and FTAs to include "demand-first, supply-second" principles</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Introduce Medium-to-Long-Term Trade Contracts</strong><br>
→ Fix import volumes 1–3 years in advance for predictable production</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Apply Environmental Costs to Trade Tariffs</strong><br>
→ Impose environmental or carbon taxes on unnecessary exports</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Build AI-Based Demand Forecast Systems</strong><br>
→ Predict international demand accurately to avoid oversupply</p>
</li>
</ul><hr><h3>6. Applicability Across All Product Categories</h3><p>This model isn’t limited to agriculture. It can be applied to all goods, including industrial products, pharmaceuticals, energy resources, and electronics.</p><p>Examples:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Pharmaceuticals</strong>: Vaccines and essential drugs produced only based on pre-orders</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Energy</strong>: Hydrogen and renewable facilities produced upon demand</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Electronics</strong>: Semiconductors and critical parts exported only per contractual need</p>
</li>
</ul><hr><h3>7. Conclusion: A Paradigm Shift for Humanity and the Planet</h3><p>The current trade structure, driven by export competition and profit, is no longer sustainable. It exacerbates environmental damage, resource depletion, and geopolitical tensions. Now is the time to shift focus from <strong>“what can we sell” to “what do others truly need.”</strong></p><p>A <strong>need-based trade model</strong> protects the Earth, promotes efficient resource use, and lays the groundwork for peace and cooperation among nations. This is the path forward for a more sustainable and harmonious world order.</p><hr><p><br></p>
